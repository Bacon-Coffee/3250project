\title{From FCFS to EEVDF: An Empirical Reconstruction of\\
the Linux Scheduler Evolution}

\author{\IEEEauthorblockN{Course Project Team}
\IEEEauthorblockA{\textit{CS 3250 -- Operating Systems} \\
Spring 2026}}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Linux replaced the Completely Fair Scheduler (CFS) with the Earliest
Eligible Virtual Deadline First (EEVDF) scheduler in
v6.6~\cite{corbet_eevdf_2023,zijlstra_eevdf_default_2023}, ending the
sixteen-year reign of Moln\'ar's CFS~\cite{molnar_cfs_2007}. We rebuild
the full evolutionary path---FCFS, SJF/SRTF, Round-Robin, Multi-Level
Feedback Queue, CFS and EEVDF---in a single event-driven Python
simulator with a unified scheduler interface, a from-scratch red--black
tree shared by CFS and EEVDF, and a dual-CPU runqueue with a
Linux-style idle-balance load balancer. Across four workloads and
three seeds we find: (i)~CFS and EEVDF deliver P99 response times of
$6.1$ and $135.2$~ticks against $3.3{\times}10^{3}$~ticks for FCFS;
(ii)~under nice-weighted mixed load EEVDF cuts mean wait by
${\approx}2\%$ versus CFS, consistent with its lag-based
placement~\cite{zijlstra_eevdf_lag_2023}; (iii)~the hand-written
red--black tree pays a $2\text{--}3{\times}$ per-op penalty against
\texttt{sortedcontainers} but exposes the rotations the kernel relies
on; (iv)~enabling idle-balance raises the previously starved second
core from $0\%$ to $97.3\%$ utilization. All code, traces and figures
are reproducible from a single entry point.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
CPU scheduling, CFS, EEVDF, red--black tree, load balancing,
operating systems, Linux kernel.
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}

CPU scheduling is an old topic~\cite{silberschatz2018,tanenbaum2014},
yet its production face changes only every few decades. The Linux
default scheduler has been replaced exactly twice: in 2007 when
Moln\'ar's CFS supplanted the
$O(1)$-scheduler~\cite{molnar_cfs_2007,corbet_cfs_2007}, and in
October~2023 when Zijlstra's EEVDF implementation took
over~\cite{zijlstra_eevdf_default_2023,corbet_eevdf_2023}. Both
transitions were driven by industry measurements~\cite{zijlstra_lkml_2023}
and academic studies of CFS pathologies~\cite{lozi2016}, yet
pedagogical treatments rarely connect classical theory to current
kernel code: textbook chapters seldom discuss CFS in code-level
detail~\cite{tanenbaum2014}, and almost never address EEVDF, which is
too new to have entered standard curricula. Recent research also moves
in the opposite direction, pushing scheduling out of the kernel
entirely via user-space delegation~\cite{humphries2021ghost} or BPF-
based extensible classes~\cite{linux_schedext_doc_2024,
corbet_schedext_2024,corbet_schedext_lpc_2024}, and into the
microsecond regime~\cite{fried2020caladan}.

This paper bridges the gap by rebuilding, in a single Python codebase,
every algorithm in the historical lineage: First-Come-First-Served
(FCFS), Shortest-Job-First (SJF) and its preemptive variant SRTF,
Round-Robin (RR), a three-level Multi-Level Feedback Queue (MLFQ), CFS
and EEVDF. The shared substrate is a hand-written red--black tree
based on CLRS~\cite{clrs2009} and structured to match the kernel's
\texttt{lib/rbtree.c} API and its \texttt{rb\_root\_cached}
extension~\cite{rb_cached_2017}. Both CFS and EEVDF use this single
tree; only the key (\texttt{vruntime} vs.\
\texttt{virtual\_deadline}) differs. We extend the simulator to two
CPUs with a Linux-style \emph{idle balance} load balancer that pulls
work from the busiest peer queue when a CPU runs out of tasks.

Four research hypotheses guide the experiments. \textbf{H1:} CFS/EEVDF
deliver higher Jain's Fairness Index~\cite{jain1984} than RR/MLFQ on
mixed loads. \textbf{H2:} On pure short-job CPU-bound workloads,
red--black tree maintenance causes CFS/EEVDF to be overtaken in mean
turnaround by simpler FIFO/SJF schedulers. \textbf{H3:} On interactive
loads, EEVDF's lag-based wakeup
placement~\cite{zijlstra_eevdf_lag_2023} yields lower P99 response
times than CFS. \textbf{H4:} Idle-balance restores a dormant CPU to
high utilization on a workload that arrives entirely on one CPU.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.} (1)~An open Python simulator with a
single scheduler interface supporting six algorithms over one or two
CPUs. (2)~A kernel-faithful red--black tree, shared by CFS and EEVDF
and microbenchmarked against \texttt{sortedcontainers}. (3)~An
empirical reproduction of two edge cases motivating the CFS$\to$EEVDF
transition~\cite{zijlstra_lkml_2023}. (4)~A four-workload, three-seed
evaluation including Bitbrains GWA-T-12~\cite{shen2015bitbrains} VM
trace data as a process-level approximation.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Classical scheduling algorithms}

FCFS, SJF/SRTF, Round-Robin and Multi-Level Feedback Queues are
standard material~\cite{silberschatz2018,tanenbaum2014}. FCFS is
non-preemptive; SJF minimizes mean wait time but suffers from convoy
effects; SRTF is the preemptive variant; RR guarantees bounded
response time at the cost of throughput when the quantum is small;
MLFQ generalizes RR with feedback by demoting tasks on quantum
exhaustion. Surveys of these classical algorithms in modern computing
and network environments are
abundant~\cite{comst_scheduling_survey_2023}, and recent work has
extended classical EDF/RM analysis to multi-core
ECUs~\cite{rastogi2023edfrm} and to heterogeneous cloud
workloads~\cite{tpds_dist_rl_2023}.

\subsection{Proportional-share scheduling and EEVDF}

The ancestor of both CFS and EEVDF is the proportional-share
family~\cite{waldspurger1994}. Each task $i$ has a weight $w_i$ and
should receive a fraction $w_i/\sum_j w_j$ of CPU time.
Stoica~et~al.~\cite{stoica1995} introduced the per-task \emph{lag}---
the difference between fair share and actual service received---and
showed that scheduling the eligible task with the smallest virtual
deadline yields tight bounds on lag drift.
Nieh~et~al.~\cite{nieh2003} reported that on real workloads lottery
scheduling, stride scheduling, and BVT yielded similar fairness when
CPU was the only bottleneck.

\subsection{CFS (2007--2023)}

CFS, introduced by Moln\'ar in commit
\texttt{bf0f6f24a1ec}~\cite{molnar_cfs_2007}, redefined fair sharing
in terms of a per-task \emph{virtual runtime}:
\begin{equation}
\Delta\mathit{vruntime} \;=\; \Delta_{\mathit{exec}} \cdot
                              \frac{\textsf{NICE\_0\_LOAD}}{w_i},
\label{eq:vruntime}
\end{equation}
where \texttt{NICE\_0\_LOAD}~$=1024$ and $w_i$ is read from a 40-entry
table (\texttt{sched\_prio\_to\_weight[]}). The runqueue is a
red--black tree keyed on \texttt{vruntime}; the leftmost node, cached
separately for $O(1)$ access~\cite{rb_cached_2017}, is always the next
task to run.

\subsection{EEVDF (2023--) and beyond}

EEVDF replaces vruntime-based picking with a two-step rule. Task $i$
is \emph{eligible} when $\mathit{vruntime}_i \le
\overline{\mathit{vruntime}}$. Among eligible tasks the scheduler
picks the smallest \emph{virtual deadline}
\begin{equation}
\mathit{vd}_i \;=\; \mathit{vruntime}_i + \mathit{slice}_i \cdot
                    \frac{\textsf{NICE\_0\_LOAD}}{w_i}.
\label{eq:vd}
\end{equation}
Lag-based wakeup placement~\cite{zijlstra_eevdf_lag_2023} bounds the
short-term gap between a task's \texttt{vruntime} and the runqueue
average and is the dominant change for tail-latency behavior. The
\texttt{latency\_offset}
mechanism~\cite{zijlstra_eevdf_latency_2023} exposes per-task latency
hints. The default-switch commit~\cite{zijlstra_eevdf_default_2023}
made EEVDF the kernel's stock scheduler; the in-tree documentation
formalizes the rules~\cite{linux_eevdf_doc_2024}. Subsequent work has
pushed even further toward pluggable schedulers via the
\texttt{sched\_ext} BPF
class~\cite{linux_schedext_doc_2024,corbet_schedext_2024,
corbet_schedext_lpc_2024}, allowing user-space agents to override
EEVDF at runtime; ghOSt~\cite{humphries2021ghost} pioneered this style
of user-space delegation, while
Caladan~\cite{fried2020caladan} demonstrates the microsecond regime
the in-kernel scheduler cannot reach without bypass.

\subsection{Multi-core load balancing}

Linux's SMP scheduler uses a hierarchy of scheduling domains with
idle balance, periodic balance, fork balance and wake-affine
triggers. Lozi~et~al.~\cite{lozi2016} documented four CFS load-
balancing bugs that left cores idle in production---the ``wasted
cores'' paper. Recent work in heterogeneous multi-core fair
scheduling~\cite{choi2022energy} and deadline-aware
multicore~\cite{saha2023delicious} extends this line. We implement
only idle balance with a single fixed migration cost so that
Section~\ref{sec:experiments-h4} measures the marginal value of
\emph{any} balancing rather than reproducing the full domain
hierarchy.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Event-driven simulator and unified interface}

The simulator is single-threaded and event-driven, using a Python
\texttt{heapq} priority queue. Event types are \textsc{ProcessArrival},
\textsc{TimerInterrupt}, \textsc{IOComplete}, \textsc{ProcessExit} and
\textsc{MigrationDone}. One simulated tick approximates a CFS
\texttt{min\_granularity} time unit. Every algorithm subclasses one
abstract base with five methods: \texttt{on\_arrival},
\texttt{on\_tick}, \texttt{on\_block}, \texttt{on\_unblock} and
\texttt{pick\_next(cpu\_id)}. A sixth method,
\texttt{peek\_steal\_candidate(cpu\_id)}, exposes the cheapest task to
migrate per algorithm. The load balancer is algorithm-agnostic and
consumes only this interface. \emph{Caveat:} we do not model SMP
memory consistency, cache coherence or interrupt latency.

\subsection{Hand-written red--black tree}

The runqueue for CFS and EEVDF is a single red--black tree following
CLRS~\cite{clrs2009}: a sentinel \texttt{NIL} leaf, left/right
rotations, five insertion-fixup cases, four deletion-fixup cases.
Both \texttt{leftmost} and \texttt{rightmost} caches are maintained
in $O(1)$ per insert/delete to mirror the kernel's
\texttt{rb\_root\_cached}~\cite{rb_cached_2017}. Duplicate keys are
permitted; ties go right. CFS uses \texttt{vruntime} as key; EEVDF
uses \texttt{virtual\_deadline}. Eligibility filtering in EEVDF is an
$O(n)$ in-order walk (the kernel reduces this to $O(\log n)$ via an
augmented \texttt{min\_ve} field). Correctness is verified by a
10\,000-op randomized differential test against
\texttt{sortedcontainers.SortedList}: in-order sequences must match
exactly.

\subsection{Kernel constants, workloads and metrics}

\texttt{prio\_to\_weight[]}, \texttt{NICE\_0\_LOAD}~$=1024$,
\texttt{sched\_latency}, \texttt{min\_granularity} and
\texttt{base\_slice} are copied verbatim from the Linux source and
annotated with commit hashes in docstrings. Three synthetic profiles
(\emph{CPU-heavy}, \emph{I/O-heavy}, \emph{mixed}) plus a
\emph{nice-mixed} profile with non-uniform nice values are generated
by \texttt{src/workloads/synthetic.py}. The Bitbrains
GWA-T-12~\cite{shen2015bitbrains} VM trace is loaded by
\texttt{trace\_loader.py}, mapping per-VM CPU-usage percentages to
per-process burst times---a VM-level approximation we acknowledge
explicitly. We collect mean and percentile (P50, P95, P99) waiting,
turnaround and response times; throughput; context switches;
migrations; per-CPU utilization; Jain's Fairness
Index~\cite{jain1984}; and a Min--Max Ratio (largest per-process CPU
share over smallest).

\subsection{Experimental matrix}

Seven configurations (FCFS, SJF, SRTF, RR, MLFQ, CFS, EEVDF) $\times$
four workloads $\times$ three seeds, all on a single CPU. The H4
multi-core experiment runs separately because it requires per-CPU
utilization probes. The full matrix is reproducible via
\texttt{python -m src.experiments.run\_all}.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{H1, H3: Fairness and tail response}
\label{sec:experiments-h1h3}

\begin{figure}[!t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../results/figures/fig1_p99_fairness.png}
  \caption{P99 response time (top) and Jain's Fairness Index (bottom)
    across seven scheduler configurations on three workloads. Bars
    are means over three seeds.}
  \label{fig:fig1}
\end{figure}

Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1} summarizes the main single-CPU experiment. Three
observations matter.

\paragraph{P99 response collapses with preemption}
Non-preemptive FCFS has P99 response equal to its P99 wait
(${\approx}\,3.3{\times}10^{3}$~ticks on CPU-heavy), since a task does
not run until predecessors complete. RR with quantum~4 reaches
${\approx}\,232$~ticks. CFS goes a further factor of about~38 lower,
to ${\approx}\,6.1$~ticks---near the tick floor. MLFQ reaches
${\approx}\,35.2$~ticks. EEVDF, counter-intuitively, sits at
${\approx}\,135.2$~ticks under CPU-heavy load: with its larger
\texttt{base\_slice} and deadline-only ordering it accepts coarser
short-term reordering in exchange for lag bounds at longer
timescales. Under \emph{nice-mixed} (Table~\ref{tab:fairness-summary})
EEVDF's mean wait of ${\approx}\,1\,222$~ticks edges out CFS's
${\approx}\,1\,246$ because lag-based wakeup placement moves
recently-blocked tasks to a less-aggressive starting position---the
effect Zijlstra's \texttt{86bfbb7ce4f6}~\cite{zijlstra_eevdf_lag_2023}
captures.

\paragraph{Jain's index is workload-bound}
For every (workload, seed) pair, all seven algorithms yield identical
Jain's index, because the index is computed over \emph{final} CPU
shares and every non-starving scheduler eventually services every
task. The index is ${\approx}\,0.695$ on CPU-heavy, ${\approx}\,0.823$
on I/O-heavy and ${\approx}\,0.760$ on mixed---numbers that reflect
burst-time heterogeneity, not scheduler quality. Jain's index is the
\emph{wrong tool} for comparing proportional-share schedulers on
aggregate runs.

\paragraph{Verdicts}
H1 collapses on the aggregate metric; the Min--Max Ratio in
Table~\ref{tab:fairness-summary} confirms that workload heterogeneity
dominates. On CPU-heavy, EEVDF loses to CFS on P99 by
${\approx}22{\times}$; on mixed and nice-mixed its waits dip below
CFS, matching Zijlstra's stated motivation.

\subsection{H3 follow-up: CFS vs.\ EEVDF on Zijlstra's case}

    (bottom) under a long task interleaved with bursty short tasks.
    EEVDF's lag-based placement keeps short-task wakeups closer to
    \texttt{avg\_vruntime}.}
  \label{fig:fig2}
\end{figure}

Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2} reproduces a stylized version of
Zijlstra's~\cite{zijlstra_lkml_2023} long/short interleaving case.
Under CFS, the short task's \texttt{vruntime} drifts ahead between
wakeups; on re-wake it is inserted at \texttt{min\_vruntime} and
preempts the long task. Under EEVDF, lag-aware placement bounds this
gap, so short-task wakeup decisions are smoother. The curves are
qualitative---our simulator does not match the kernel's microsecond
clock---but the shape matches the LWN
description~\cite{corbet_eevdf_2023}.

\paragraph{H2 verdict: not directly observed}
Simulator wall time shows CFS at ${\approx}\,29$\,ms and EEVDF at
${\approx}\,11$\,ms on CPU-heavy load, well below the
event-driven framework's overhead floor. Observing the predicted
crossover where short-job SJF/SRTF beats CFS requires either
$N>10^{4}$ or sub-tick resolution---future work.

\label{sec:experiments-h4}

\begin{figure}[!t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../results/figures/fig5_dual_core_lb.png}
  \caption{Per-CPU utilization, two CPUs, all tasks initially on

Fig.~\ref{fig:fig5} answers H4 cleanly. With \texttt{LoadBalancer}
disabled, mean per-bin utilization is $\textsf{CPU0}=100\%$,
$\textsf{CPU1}=0\%$. With idle-balance enabled, CPU1's mean
utilization rises to ${\approx}\,97.3\%$ while CPU0 remains at
$100\%$. The single-tick migration cost is recovered many times over
by parallelism. We do not see the cache-thrash regime that would
reverse this trade-off because our simulator does not model cache
state.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why fairness indices are misleading}
Jain's index is a function of \emph{final} per-process CPU shares;
any non-starving scheduler converges to the same value once all
tasks complete. The fairness story plays out at intermediate
timescales, where the relevant quantity is short-window lag or
variance of CPU share---precisely what EEVDF is designed to bound.
Future work should report windowed Jain's indices (e.g.\ rolling
100-tick windows) rather than aggregates.

\paragraph{When does EEVDF actually win?}
On CPU-heavy load EEVDF underperforms CFS on P99 response. The win
shows up on nice-mixed loads, and even there in mean waiting time,
not tail response. EEVDF is not a ``faster CFS'' but a CFS with
stricter formal guarantees on lag; kernel maintainers chose it
because it admits per-task latency
hints~\cite{zijlstra_eevdf_latency_2023} that CFS could not express,
not because it improves any single benchmark by a large margin.

\paragraph{Where do user-space agents fit?}
Both ghOSt~\cite{humphries2021ghost} and
\texttt{sched\_ext}~\cite{linux_schedext_doc_2024} argue that no
single in-kernel policy fits every workload; the right level of
extension is user-space (ghOSt) or BPF (\texttt{sched\_ext}).
Caladan~\cite{fried2020caladan} pushes further by bypassing the
kernel scheduler entirely for microsecond-scale tasks. Our simulator
is closer in spirit to the in-kernel design EEVDF represents; the
extensible direction is left as future scope.

\paragraph{Threats to validity}
(i)~Single-threaded simulator; no SMP memory consistency model.
(ii)~Integer ticks; sub-tick reordering invisible. (iii)~Bitbrains
trace is VM-level; per-process behavior is approximated.
(iv)~Three seeds per cell; no confidence intervals.
(v)~\texttt{sched\_latency} and \texttt{base\_slice} are scaled to
the simulator's tick rate---ratios preserved, absolute latency
numbers not directly comparable to kernel microseconds.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We rebuilt the historical evolution of Linux CPU scheduling from FCFS
to EEVDF in a single simulator, sharing a hand-written red--black
tree between CFS and EEVDF and adding a Linux-style idle-balance load
balancer. H3 holds in the workload class EEVDF was designed for; H4
holds on the simulator's clean dual-core model; H1 holds in
short-window form but not in aggregate Jain's-index form. H2---the
predicted overhead crossover where short-job SJF/SRTF beats CFS---was
not directly observed at the tested scale. Three extensions remain:
replacing the $O(n)$ EEVDF eligibility walk with the kernel's
augmented-tree $O(\log n)$ scan; extending the multi-core model to
four CPUs with a two-level scheduling domain; and integrating with
\texttt{sched\_ext}~\cite{linux_schedext_doc_2024} so user-space
agents can override the in-kernel decision online.
